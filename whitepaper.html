§ 1What This Tool Does — and What It Doesn't

Couples therapy is physiologically demanding. John Gottman and Robert Levenson's landmark research beginning in the 1970s demonstrated that escalating conflict drives measurable changes in both partners' autonomic nervous systems — heart rate rises, heart rate variability collapses, and the capacity for empathic listening narrows sharply [1, 2]. When a partner's heart rate exceeds their personal flooding threshold, they can no longer process information effectively. Continued conversation at that point typically makes things worse, not better [3].

This tool brings that physiology into the room as a visible, shared signal. It is designed to help you recognize the early signs of physiological flooding before the conversation derails, to support a genuinely effective recovery break when needed, and to give you a richer picture of how each partner's body is responding throughout the session.

The signal is physiological, not emotional Everything this tool measures is a body-state proxy. A rising heart rate may reflect anger, excitement, effort, deep engagement, or a passing intrusive thought. The numbers describe what the body is doing — they do not tell you what a person is feeling, whether a relationship is healthy, or what is causing the arousal. Your relational attunement remains the primary interpretive instrument. The physiology is a second channel of information, not a replacement for the first.

§ 2Before the Session: Hardware and Setup

Choosing a sensor

Any Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) heart rate monitor that follows the standard Heart Rate Service specification works with this system — chest straps, armbands, or optical finger sensors. Chest straps generally provide more accurate R–R interval data than wrist-based optical sensors because they measure the electrical signal directly rather than inferring it from blood volume changes under the skin. For couples work, an unobtrusive chest strap (worn under clothing) is usually the least disruptive option. Make sure both devices are charged before the session.

Starting the application

  1. Open a terminal in the application folder and activate the virtual environment:
    source .venv/bin/activate
  2. Start the server: python launch.py
    The browser will open automatically to the setup dialog.
  3. Both partners put on their sensors. Give them 1–2 minutes to settle before proceeding so the optical or electrical contact stabilizes.
  4. In the setup dialog, click Scan for devices. The app searches for nearby BLE heart rate monitors for 10 seconds and lists what it finds by device name and identifier. If you have the device identifiers written on the sensors (recommended), match them in the dropdowns. Type each partner's name into the name fields and click Start session.
Signal quality A small colored dot appears next to each partner's name. Green means the sensor is reading cleanly (≥ 90% of beats usable). Amber means acceptable but imperfect — check that the strap is snug and the sensor pad is making skin contact. Red means the signal is too noisy to trust; reposition the sensor before proceeding. Signal quality often improves after the first minute of wear as the sensor warms up.

Voice enrollment (optional)

The session transcript feature identifies who is speaking in real time. For speaker identification to work, each person speaks alone into the microphone for about four seconds — their name button in the voice enrollment row turns to a checkmark when enrolled. Do this before starting the recording. The therapist can also enroll their own voice if they want their own utterances attributed.

§ 3Collecting the Baseline

Every comparison this tool makes is individual — it measures each partner against their own calm resting state, not against population averages. Population norms for heart rate variability vary enormously with age, fitness, medications, chronic stress, and time of day; applying a fixed cut-off would frequently mislead [4]. The baseline you collect at the start of each session sets the personal reference point for all metrics that follow.

Baseline procedure

  1. Once sensors are showing green signal quality, ask both partners to sit quietly and breathe normally. No specific instructions about breathing rate — just settled, ordinary sitting.
  2. Wait at least two full minutes. The nervous system needs time to shift toward resting tone; a baseline captured while someone is still talking or moving will set the reference too high and compress all subsequent comparisons.
  3. Click set baseline — A and set baseline — B. The app uses the preceding 2–5 minutes of data, sliding a one-minute window in 30-second steps to build a distribution for each metric rather than a single point estimate. The baseline indicator in the header confirms when both are set.
If the state description reads "moderate" at rest This usually means the baseline was captured when a partner was still physiologically elevated — perhaps from the drive over, anticipation of the session, or anxiety about the sensors. Clear the baseline with the "clear — A / B" button, wait a few minutes, and reset. A well-collected baseline should produce a resting state description of "low activation, stable."

Baselines can be cleared and reset at any point in the session — for example, if a partner needed to get up and move around, or if you want to establish a new reference point after a successful recovery break.

§ 4Reading the Session Dashboard

The main session view shows both partners side by side. Here is what each element tells you and what to watch for.

The activation score and time series (0–100)

The large number under each partner's heart rate is their activation index — a composite of all physiological signals expressed as a single 0–100 score against their personal baseline. Think of it as a body-state thermometer. Below it, a scrolling time series shows the last three minutes of activation history, moving from left to right. Labels along the bottom show the time scale (−3m, −2m, −1m, now).

ZoneScoreColorWhat it means clinically
Calm0–35Teal Physiologically settled. Capacity for empathic listening and reflective processing is intact.
Moderate35–65Amber Elevated arousal — engaged, mildly stressed, or emotionally involved. Watch the direction: rising is an early warning, falling is positive.
High65–100Red Strong physiological activation. If the flooding threshold is also crossed, a break is indicated.

The direction arrow next to the score (rising / stable / falling) is often more useful than the score itself. A partner at 55 and rising is heading somewhere different from a partner at 55 and falling.

Confidence — when signals disagree When the activation score is followed by "(signals mixed)," it means the physiological indicators are pointing in different directions — heart rate is elevated, say, but heart rhythm variability and coherence remain normal. This is a signal to wait for more data rather than act on the score. Confidence typically resolves within one or two update cycles (20–30 seconds).

Heart rate

The large number in each partner panel is current heart rate in beats per minute, updated with every heartbeat. The delta below it shows the percentage above or below the personal baseline mean — so "+14% above baseline" tells you something more useful than the raw number. The heart rate turns red and the panel is highlighted when the flooding threshold is crossed (see §5).

Heart rhythm variability (HRV)

Labeled "HRV" in the tile below the canvas, the value shown (in milliseconds) is RMSSD — the standard clinical measure of beat-to-beat variability. Higher values mean the heart is varying more freely between beats, which reflects active parasympathetic (vagal) regulation. Under emotional stress, the vagus nerve withdraws tone quickly — within a few beats — and RMSSD drops. When a person genuinely calms down, vagal tone returns and RMSSD rises. This is one of the earliest and most sensitive indicators of stress onset and recovery [5, 6].

Vagal activity

This tile measures how much of the heart's variability falls within the breathing-frequency range — the physiological signature of the vagal brake being applied in coordination with each breath [7]. When someone is stressed and breathing irregularly, this number falls. When they are breathing slowly and steadily, it rises. During recovery breathing exercises, watching vagal activity rise confirms that the paced breathing is engaging the parasympathetic system, not just passing time.

Respiration rate

Derived from the heart rate signal rather than a dedicated sensor — the heart rate accelerates slightly with each inhalation and slows with each exhalation, making breathing rate readable in the heart signal [8]. This is a useful rough guide during recovery breathing (confirming the partner is hitting the target rate) but should not be treated as a precise measurement during natural conversation, when breathing is irregular.

Coherence

Coherence measures how organized the heart rhythm is, not just how much it varies. A high-coherence rhythm shows a single, smooth oscillation — the physiological signature of a self-regulated state [9]. A low-coherence rhythm is fragmented and irregular. Coherence often drops before heart rate rises visibly — it can be an early warning of stress that arrives before the conscious experience of feeling stressed. The bar graph fills from left to right; a rising bar during a recovery break is the clearest physiological confirmation that down-regulation is actually occurring.

State description

Below the partner name, a short plain-language sentence describes what the body is doing: the activation level, direction, heart rate relative to baseline, and a note about breathing or vagal tone when these are notable. These sentences describe body facts, not emotions — "heart rate 82 bpm — 18% above baseline" is a body fact. What that means emotionally is your interpretation, informed by what is happening in the conversation.

The dyadic coupling panel

At the bottom of the main view, the dyadic panel shows whether the two partners' physiologies are moving together. Peak r is a correlation coefficient (−1 to +1); values above 0.4 indicate meaningful physiological linkage. Lag / leader shows which partner's physiology tends to precede the other's by a few seconds.

The critical insight — supported by research on interpersonal emotion systems [10, 11] — is that synchrony must be read against arousal state:

What you seeWhat it likely means
High synchrony, both partners calm Physiological co-regulation — a positive sign of attunement
High synchrony, both partners activated or flooded Locked in conflict — physiologies amplifying each other's stress
Anti-phase synchrony One partner's activation is coupled with the other's suppression — or vice versa
Low or absent synchrony Physiologically parallel but not linked in this window
Dyadic coupling is exploratory The coupling panel is marked "exploratory layer" for a reason. Some statistical correlation between two people in the same room is expected even for strangers — a loud sound or a shared laugh will move both physiologies. Without a comparison against a non-partner baseline, exact r values are hard to interpret in isolation. Use the panel as a qualitative indicator of relational dynamics, not a diagnostic measure.

§ 5When Flooding Is Detected

Gottman's concept of Diffuse Physiological Arousal (DPA) — which he termed "flooding" — describes a state in which sympathetic activation is so high that the partner's physiological system is overridden [3, 12]. At this level of arousal, the capacity for empathic listening collapses, the ability to access nuanced memory is impaired, and the body is in a mobilized state oriented toward self-protection rather than connection. Continued conversation in this state is rarely productive and can deepen negative affect patterns.

How flooding is detected

The app flags flooding when a partner's heart rate meets either of two criteria:

  • Personal threshold: heart rate is 10% or more above the individual resting baseline mean. This is the primary criterion and captures individual differences — a person whose resting heart rate is 58 bpm floods at a different absolute level than one whose resting rate is 80 bpm.
  • Absolute ceiling: heart rate reaches 95 bpm or above, which serves as a floor for those with already-elevated baselines.

To avoid false alarms from momentary spikes — a cough, a shift in posture, a single irregular beat — the flooding alert requires the threshold to be sustained for at least ten continuous seconds before triggering. It clears automatically when heart rate returns below the threshold.

When the flood alert appears A red banner names the flooded partner and offers a one-click link to the recovery screen. Gottman's research suggests that norepinephrine — the primary stress neurochemical activated during flooding — takes approximately 20 minutes to clear, even after the stressor stops [3]. A break shorter than that often fails to produce genuine physiological recovery, and the conversation resumes from an essentially unchanged physiological state.

What to do

When flooding is detected, treat it as a clinical signal rather than a crisis. Name what the body is showing without alarming language — something like: "I'm noticing that your body is working hard right now. Let's take a genuine break before we continue." Navigate to the recovery screen together. Both partners should participate in the breathing exercise — co-regulated physiological recovery is itself a relational act [13], and the dyadic synchrony data often shows this clearly.

§ 6The Recovery Break

The recovery screen (press r or click "recovery mode →") is a dedicated physiological break tool. It offers paced breathing with a visual animation, ambient sound, and live coherence feedback for both partners. The goal is genuine autonomic down-regulation — a measurable shift in the physiology — not just a pause in talking.

Breathing patterns

PatternRhythmRateRationale
Coherence (recommended) 5.5 s in / 5.5 s out ~5.5 breaths/min Near the cardiovascular resonance frequency — maximizes heart rhythm coherence and vagal tone [14, 15]. The most reliable physiological down-regulator.
Box 4 s in / 4 s hold / 4 s out / 4 s hold ~4 breaths/min Equal-phase structure supports focus and stabilization. Useful when partners are finding it hard to slow down enough for coherence breathing.
4-7-8 4 s in / 7 s hold / 8 s out ~3 breaths/min Extended exhalation strongly activates the parasympathetic system [16]. Best for high-activation states where coherence breathing alone isn't slowing the physiology.

The breathing circle expands and contracts with the target pattern. A countdown shows remaining seconds in each phase. Ambient pink noise can be toggled on — its natural sound texture supports attentional defocusing, which itself helps shift the nervous system away from the ruminative rehearsal that sustains flooding [17].

Reading recovery progress

Coherence typically takes 2–3 minutes of paced breathing to rise noticeably. Expect the first cycle or two to show little change — the body needs time to entrain. Watch the coherence bars and trend arrows (↑ / → / ↓) for both partners. Rising coherence is the most direct confirmation that the breathing exercise is engaging the autonomic system.

When both partners' activation scores drop below 35 and stay there, a calm zone timer appears on screen showing how long that sustained regulation has held. This timer — not the wall clock — is the readiness signal. A partner who has been in the calm zone for 2–3 minutes is physiologically ready to re-engage with difficult material. One who has been sitting quietly for 20 minutes but is still at activation 60 is not.

If coherence isn't rising after 5+ minutes Rumination about the unresolved conflict is likely maintaining the sympathetic state. Fredrickson and Levenson's research showed that positive emotions — even mild ones — meaningfully accelerate cardiovascular recovery from stress [18]. A brief structured distraction (a simple shared task, a change of room, a neutral topic) is often more effective than continued breathing when rumination is keeping the physiology elevated.

§ 7After the Session: The Session Log

The app continuously records the session to a structured log file. Everything you need to review what happened is captured there. To download it, click the ↓ download log button that appears in the transcript panel once recording has begun.

What the log contains

The session log is a JSON file that includes three layers of information:

  • Transcript events. Each speech segment is recorded with the speaker's name, the text, and a snapshot of both partners' full physiological state at the moment the utterance ended. A second snapshot captured 15 seconds later records the autonomic response to what was said — the body reacts to emotionally significant speech on a 5–30 second timescale, so this response window captures the beginning of that reaction.
  • Metric snapshots. Every 30 seconds throughout the session, a complete physiological record is written — heart rate, heart rhythm variability, vagal activity, coherence, respiration rate, activation score, confidence, and the state description for each partner. These give you a full time series of the session's physiological arc, even during silences between utterances.
  • Lifecycle events. Timestamped records of every significant event: when sensors connected or disconnected, when baselines were set or cleared, when voice enrollment occurred, and when flooding started and ended (including the heart rate and percentage above baseline at each transition).

The downloaded file is readable by any tool that handles JSON — it can be opened in a spreadsheet, analyzed with Python or R, or submitted to an AI assistant for summary and pattern identification. The metric guide embedded in the file explains every field for any downstream reader.

Reviewing with an AI assistant The session log is structured for AI-assisted analysis. You can share it with Claude or a similar assistant and ask questions like: "Which utterances were followed by the largest physiological response? When during the session was Partner A regulated while Partner B was activated? How long did the recovery break take to bring both partners into the calm zone?" The response window data is especially useful — it reveals which moments in the conversation produced significant body reactions, independent of what either partner reported feeling.

§ 8The Science Behind the Signals

Why heart rate variability matters

The heart does not beat like a metronome. Even at a steady average rate, the interval between successive beats varies continuously — speeding slightly with each inhalation, slowing with each exhalation, and fluctuating in response to emotional, cognitive, and social inputs. This variability is not noise; it is the signature of an active, adaptive nervous system. Loss of variability — a flat, metronomic rhythm — reflects a system under stress or constraint [5].

The parasympathetic (vagal) branch of the autonomic nervous system is the primary driver of this variability on the beat-to-beat timescale. When we feel safe and connected, the vagus nerve actively modulates the heart, producing the rich variability that enables responsive, nuanced social engagement [19]. Under threat or stress, vagal tone is withdrawn rapidly — within a few beats — and the sympathetic system takes over, raising heart rate and suppressing variability in preparation for mobilization.

Stephen Porges's polyvagal theory provides a framework for understanding why this matters in couples therapy specifically [19]: the same vagal system that produces heart rhythm variability also regulates the facial muscles of expression, the muscles of the middle ear (that attune to human voices), and the prosodic qualities of the voice. When vagal tone is high, partners can hear each other's emotional nuance, read facial expressions accurately, and modulate their own affect. When vagal tone is withdrawn under stress, all of these capacities narrow. Physiological regulation is not separate from relational presence — it is its substrate.

Flooding and the biology of a break

Gottman's research defined flooding as the state in which sympathetic arousal is so high that organized, empathic responding becomes physiologically impossible [3]. The mechanism is well-established: the sympathetic nervous system releases epinephrine and norepinephrine, which accelerate heart rate, suppress digestive and immune function, and shift attentional processing toward threat detection. Cognitive flexibility, accurate mentalizing, and the capacity to hear nuance all depend on prefrontal-limbic circuits that are progressively downregulated under high sympathetic load [12].

The reason a 20-minute break is specified — rather than a 5-minute one — is pharmacological. Norepinephrine has a biological half-life of several minutes in the bloodstream, and its effects on cognition persist longer than its circulating concentration. A couple who pauses for five minutes and then resumes conversation is often resuming from an essentially unchanged physiological state. The calm-zone timer in the recovery screen exists precisely to make this visible: conversation should not resume until the physiology has actually shifted, not merely paused.

Cardiac coherence and resonance breathing

Cardiac coherence refers to the degree to which the heart rhythm oscillates in a single, organized frequency rather than fragmented, multi-frequency variation. Rollin McCraty and colleagues at the HeartMath Institute established that high coherence — a smooth, large-amplitude rhythm oscillation — reflects a coordinated state of the heart-brain system associated with reduced cortisol, improved cognitive flexibility, and greater emotional regulation [9].

The most reliable way to raise coherence deliberately is resonance breathing — slow paced breathing near 5–6 breaths per minute, which matches the natural resonant frequency of the cardiovascular system. At this rate, each breath cycle synchronizes with the natural oscillation of blood pressure and heart rate, producing a large-amplitude, coherent heart rhythm oscillation that maximizes vagal activity [14, 15]. Heart rate variability biofeedback at this frequency has a substantial evidence base for reducing anxiety, improving emotional regulation, and supporting recovery from stress [20].

Dyadic physiology and co-regulation

The observation that people in close relationships show correlated physiological states — known as physiological linkage or coregulation — has been replicated across many research groups [10, 11, 13]. The quality of that linkage differs importantly between distressed and non-distressed couples: in healthy couples, physiological coupling tends to be mild, flexible, and bidirectional — a gentle social regulation. In distressed couples, coupling under conflict can become rigid and amplifying, with each partner's escalation feeding the other's [2, 21].

This distinction is what the dyadic coupling panel is designed to illuminate. High synchrony during a calm period is a different signal from high synchrony during conflict. The latter — where both physiologies are escalating together — is the pattern Gottman described as the physiological substrate of gridlocked, unresolvable conflict [3].

§ 9What the Tool Cannot Tell You

Research prototype — not a validated clinical instrument The thresholds, algorithms, and interpretive frameworks in this application represent reasonable implementations of published methods. They have not been validated in a clinical trial with couples therapy populations. Do not treat scores as diagnostic criteria or use them to make clinical decisions independent of therapist judgment.

Body signals are not emotion labels

"High activation, rising" means the body is in a mobilized physiological state. It does not distinguish excitement from anger, effort from anxiety, or the physiological correlates of intense positive engagement from those of distress. The direction and context of the activation — what was just said, what topic is active, what each partner's face and voice are doing — is necessary to interpret the number. Never read a score to a client as a verdict about their emotional state.

Sensors and movement

Consumer Bluetooth heart rate monitors use optical or electrical sensors that are susceptible to movement artifact. If a partner shifts in their seat, laughs vigorously, or the strap slips, signal quality will momentarily degrade and metrics will become unreliable. The signal quality indicator tells you when to discount the numbers; the flooding alert requires ten seconds of sustained threshold-crossing specifically to reduce the impact of momentary artifacts.

Individual biological variation

Heart rate variability declines with age, is suppressed by beta-blockers and certain antidepressants, varies with fitness, menstrual cycle phase, caffeine, and chronic stress. The personalized baseline adjusts for each partner's state at the start of that session, but does not account for medication effects or day-to-day biological variation. A partner on a beta-blocker will show compressed HRV values that have different meaning than the same values in an unmedicated person.

Respiration measurement

Breathing rate is inferred from the heart rate signal, not measured directly. This estimate is unreliable when someone is speaking continuously (because speech disrupts the normal breathing pattern), when breathing very slowly (below 9 breaths per minute, the signal migrates out of the detectable frequency range), or when heart rhythm variability is very low (there is less signal to detect). Use it as a guide during recovery breathing exercises, not as a clinical measurement during conversation.

Dyadic coupling interpretation

Cross-correlation measures statistical association, not causal influence. Two people in the same room will show some physiological correlation simply from shared environmental events — a sound outside, a change in room temperature, a shift in the conversation's emotional tone affecting both simultaneously. Without comparison against a non-partner baseline from the same session, a specific correlation value cannot be interpreted rigorously. The dyadic panel is most useful as a qualitative indicator of relational dynamics and trajectory, not as a precise measurement.
