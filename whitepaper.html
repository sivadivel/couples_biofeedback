§ 1Purpose and Clinical Context

Couples therapy is physiologically demanding. Research by John Gottman and Robert Levenson beginning in the 1970s established that escalating conflict drives measurable changes in both partners' autonomic nervous systems — heart rate rises, heart rate variability collapses, and the capacity for empathic listening narrows sharply [1, 2]. When heart rate exceeds a personal flooding threshold, partners can no longer process information effectively, and continued conversation typically makes things worse rather than better [3].

This application puts real-time physiological data in the room. It is designed for a therapist to monitor both partners' arousal states on a shared screen, to recognize the early signs of physiological flooding before the conversation derails, and to guide partners through a structured physiological break when needed. The recovery screen provides paced breathing exercises with live coherence feedback to facilitate genuine nervous system down-regulation — not just a pause in talking.

Scope of the signal All metrics are physiological proxies. They measure body state, not emotion or relational quality. A rising heart rate may reflect anger, excitement, effort, or a passing thought. Interpret signals in context and in conversation with the partners — never as independent verdicts about what someone is feeling.

§ 2System Architecture

The application follows a three-layer architecture: sensor hardware, a Python server, and a browser-based dashboard.

Hardware layer

Any Bluetooth Low Energy heart rate monitor that implements the standard Heart Rate Service (UUID 0x180D) is compatible — chest straps, armbands, or finger sensors. The device streams R–R intervals (the time between successive heartbeats) in milliseconds. Battery level is read at startup via the Battery Service (UUID 0x180F).

Server layer

A lightweight Python server (server.py) handles BLE connections via the bleak library and runs a aiohttp HTTP and WebSocket server. Heart rate metrics are computed in processor.py using a tiered cadence:

TierCadenceWindowWhat it computes
FastEvery beat10 sHeart rate (bpm)
MidEvery 5 s30 sHRV (RMSSD), flooding flag, signal quality
SlowEvery 10 s60 sVagal activity (HF), coherence, respiration rate, activation index
DyadicEvery 15 s60 sInter-partner HR synchrony

Updates are broadcast over WebSocket to all connected browser tabs. The session supports one or two partners. In single-partner mode the dyadic panel is hidden.

Client layer

A single-page web application (index.html for the session view, mode_b.html for the recovery screen) receives WebSocket messages and renders metrics in real time using the Canvas API and SVG animations. No external JavaScript frameworks are used. The app works on any modern browser and is optimized for landscape tablet display.

§ 3Signal Acquisition and Artifact Rejection

R–R intervals

Heart rate monitors transmit the time elapsed between consecutive heartbeats — the R–R or inter-beat interval (IBI), measured in milliseconds. This is more informative than beats-per-minute because it preserves the natural variability between beats that reflects autonomic nervous system activity. A healthy resting adult at 60 bpm has R–R intervals near 1000 ms; the same person at 80 bpm has intervals near 750 ms. The variation around those values — changing beat by beat — is the raw material for all HRV metrics.

Artifact rejection

Before any metric is computed, the raw R–R series passes through a two-stage cleaning filter (clean_rr()):

  1. Absolute range filter. Intervals outside 300–2000 ms (i.e., implying a heart rate outside 30–200 bpm) are discarded as sensor artifacts or movement artefacts.
  2. Ectopic beat filter. Any interval differing from the running 5-beat local median by more than 20% is flagged as an ectopic or missed beat and removed. The 20% threshold is a widely-used default [4]; conversational couples move and gesture, so the filter is intentionally permissive.

The proportion of beats that survive cleaning is reported as signal quality (0–100%, shown as a colored dot in each partner's header). Values above 90% indicate reliable contact; 75–90% is acceptable; below 75% warrants repositioning the sensor.

Heart Rhythm Variability (HRV) in depth

RMSSD — the root mean square of successive R–R differences — is the most widely validated ultra-short HRV metric. It captures rapid, beat-to-beat variability that is almost entirely mediated by the parasympathetic (vagal) branch of the autonomic nervous system [5]. Because it does not require spectral decomposition, it is reliable on windows as short as 30 seconds [6], making it well-suited to the dynamic conversational context of couples therapy.

Under stress or activation, vagal tone is withdrawn quickly — often within a few beats. RMSSD drops in parallel. When a person calms down, vagal tone returns and RMSSD rises. In the dashboard, RMSSD is compared against each person's individual baseline; the state description reports whether variability is reduced ("vagal tone withdrawn") or elevated ("parasympathetic active").

Vagal Activity (HF power) in depth

The high-frequency (HF) band of the R–R power spectrum — 0.15 to 0.40 Hz — corresponds to the range of normal resting breathing rates (9 to 24 breaths per minute). Heart rate accelerates slightly with each inhalation and slows with each exhalation; this rhythmic coupling is called Respiratory Sinus Arrhythmia (RSA) and is mediated almost exclusively by the vagus nerve [7]. HF power quantifies the amplitude of this oscillation.

Units and display scaling

The app uses a normalized Lomb-Scargle periodogram (normalize=True in SciPy), which rescales the spectrum so that a pure sinusoid of unit amplitude has a periodogram value of 1.0. After integrating over the 0.25 Hz HF band, the resulting values are typically in the range 0.0001–0.005 — far too small to display as integers. The dashboard therefore multiplies the raw value by 10,000 before display. Typical resting values on screen are roughly 5–50 a.u.; values above ~30 indicate healthy vagal activity at rest. The label "a.u." (arbitrary units) is used because the normalization removes the physical ms² dimension that would apply to an un-normalized Welch periodogram. Only relative changes within a session — not absolute values between sessions or people — should be interpreted.

Breathing rate caveat If a partner breathes slower than 9 breaths per minute — common during deep coherence breathing — the RSA peak migrates into the low-frequency (LF) band and HF power underestimates vagal tone. The activation algorithm accounts for this by switching to RMSSD-only vagal scoring when respiration drops below 9 breaths/min.

The Lomb-Scargle periodogram is used instead of the FFT because it operates directly on the unevenly sampled R–R event series without requiring cubic spline interpolation to a uniform time grid — important for short, noisy windows typical in a therapy session [8].

Respiration Rate (derived)

Because this application uses heart rate monitors rather than dedicated respiration sensors, breathing rate is inferred from the heart rate signal: the dominant spectral peak within the HF band identifies the frequency at which the heart is being rhythmically modulated by breathing. This estimate is reliable when the person is breathing in a regular pattern; it can be unreliable when talking continuously (irregular breathing) or when breathing very slowly (peak migrates out of the HF band). It is best read as a rough guide and confirmation of paced breathing during recovery, not a precise clinical measurement.

§ 5Metrics — Composite Activation Index

The activation index (displayed as a 0–100 score) is a composite of all slow-tier metrics expressed as z-scores against the individual baseline. It answers the question: How physiologically activated is this person relative to their own calm resting state?

How it is computed

Each metric is standardized against the personal baseline (mean and standard deviation collected during a calm period at the start of the session). The signs are chosen so that all components move in the same direction under arousal:

ComponentDirection under activationWeight
Heart rate↑ rises1.0
Vagal tone (RMSSD / HF)↓ falls → contributes positively1.0
Respiration rate↑ rises0.5
Coherence↓ falls → contributes positively0.5

The weighted mean of z-scores is passed through a logistic function to produce a bounded 0–100 score:

score = 100 / (1 + e−raw / 1.5)

A temporal smoothing parameter (α = 0.3) blends the new score with the previous value to reduce noise-driven jumping: score = 0.3 × new + 0.7 × previous.

Zone thresholds

ZoneScore rangeColorInterpretation
Low / calm0–35Teal Physiologically settled. Capacity for empathic listening is intact.
Moderate35–65Amber Elevated arousal — engaged or mildly stressed. Monitor for escalation.
High / flooded65–100Red Strong activation. When combined with a flooding flag, a break is indicated.

Confidence and direction

The confidence value (0–1) measures signal agreement: when all contributing components point in the same direction, confidence is 1.0. When they are mixed — say, heart rate is elevated but coherence and HRV are normal — confidence falls and the state description includes "(signals mixed)". Low confidence suggests waiting for more data before acting on the score.

The direction indicator (rising / stable / falling) is computed by comparing the current smoothed score to the previous: a change of more than 2 points triggers rising or falling.

Calm zone

When activation stays continuously below 35, a timer appears below the score showing how long that partner has been in the calm zone (e.g., "1:42 in calm zone"). This is the most clinically relevant signal on the recovery screen: physiological down-regulation has actually occurred, not just a pause.

§ 6Metrics — Flooding Detector

The flooding (Diffuse Physiological Arousal, DPA) flag is based on John Gottman's research identifying heart rate thresholds above which productive conversation becomes physiologically impossible [3]. It is computed in the mid tier (every 5 s) and triggers immediately, without smoothing.

Two-criterion rule

A partner is flagged as flooded if either criterion is met:

  1. Relative criterion: current heart rate ≥ 110% of the personal baseline mean (i.e., 10% above resting). This is the primary criterion and the most sensitive to individual differences.
  2. Absolute criterion: heart rate ≥ 95 bpm. This acts as a floor for people whose baseline is already moderately elevated.
When flooding is detected A red alert banner appears across the top of the session view naming the flooded partner(s) and offering a one-click link to the recovery screen. Research suggests that norepinephrine — the primary stress hormone during flooding — takes approximately 20 minutes to clear, even after the stressor stops [3]. A break shorter than that often fails to achieve genuine physiological recovery.

When flooding is detected, the partner's heart rate display turns red and their panel is highlighted. The "flooded" badge replaces "regulated" in their header. The flooding state automatically clears when heart rate returns below the threshold.

§ 7Metrics — Cardiac Coherence

Cardiac coherence is distinct from HRV: it is not about how much the heart rhythm varies, but about how organized that variation is. A coherent heart rhythm shows a single smooth, dominant oscillation — characteristically near 0.1 Hz (6 breaths per minute, the resonant frequency of the cardiovascular system). A disorganized rhythm shows irregular, multi-frequency variation.

McCraty coherence ratio

The app computes coherence using the method developed by Rollin McCraty and colleagues at the HeartMath Institute [9]:

  1. The R–R series is resampled to a uniform 4 Hz grid.
  2. Welch's method produces a power spectral density estimate over 0.04–0.26 Hz.
  3. The peak frequency is identified.
  4. Power within ±0.015 Hz of that peak is integrated.
  5. Coherence = peak band power ÷ (total power − peak band power).

Values above approximately 0.6 indicate an organized, self-regulated state. Values near 0 indicate a disorganized, fragmented rhythm. The coherence bar in the dashboard is scaled to a maximum of 3.0 (which represents very high coherence in practice).

Coherence during recovery

Coherence is the primary biofeedback target on the recovery screen. Slow paced breathing near 5–6 breaths per minute — the resonance frequency — naturally entrains heart rate into a large-amplitude, single-frequency oscillation, dramatically raising coherence. The recovery screen shows per-partner coherence bars with trend arrows (↑ / → / ↓) updated every 10 seconds, allowing both partners to see in real time whether the breathing exercise is producing genuine physiological regulation.

§ 8Metrics — Dyadic Coupling

The dyadic coupling panel — marked "exploratory layer" — measures the degree to which the two partners' heart rates are moving together in time. This is distinct from whether either individual is regulated; it is a relational signal about physiological co-regulation or co-dysregulation.

Time-lagged cross-correlation

Every 15 seconds, the app computes a windowed time-lagged cross-correlation (Pearson r) between the two partners' instantaneous heart rate series over the preceding 60 seconds [10]. Both series are:

  1. Interpolated onto a common 4 Hz uniform time grid.
  2. Linearly detrended to remove shared slow drift that would inflate apparent synchrony.
  3. Normalized to zero mean and unit variance.

The lag that produces the maximum absolute correlation is reported, along with its sign (which partner leads) and the in-phase / anti-phase classification.

The three dyadic measures

MeasureWhat it shows
Peak r Maximum Pearson correlation over all tested lags (±4 s). Range −1 to +1. Values near ±1 indicate tight physiological coupling; near 0 indicates independent physiologies.
Lag / leader The time offset (in seconds) at which the peak correlation occurs, and which partner's physiology tends to lead the other. Positive lag = Partner A leads; negative = Partner B leads.
Phase In-phase: the partners' heart rates rise and fall together. Anti-phase: one rises while the other falls.

Interpreting dyadic signals

The interpretation sentence beneath the dyadic stats reflects the critical insight from Butler, Randall, Reed, and colleagues [11, 12]: synchrony must be read against arousal state, not in isolation.

SynchronyArousal stateInterpretation
High (r ≥ 0.4), in-phase Both settled Co-regulation signal — physiologies entraining in a healthy direction.
High (r ≥ 0.4) Both flooded Locked in conflict — synchronized arousal amplification, not co-regulation.
High (r ≥ 0.4), anti-phase Any Oppositional coupling — one partner's activation suppresses the other's, or vice versa.
Weak (r 0.2–0.4) Any Loose linkage — physiologically present in the same room but not tightly coupled.
Absent (r < 0.2) Any Independent physiologies — no detectable coupling in this window.
Surrogate baseline note Some degree of cross-correlation between two people in the same room is expected even for strangers, arising from shared environmental events (a loud sound, a change in room temperature). To establish what counts as "meaningful" coupling for a specific pair, compare against surrogate pairs — non-partner recordings from the same session, or shuffled temporal segments. This is not currently automated in the app and represents an important limitation of the exploratory layer.

§ 9Personalized Baselining

Every threshold and comparison in this app is individual. Population norms for HRV vary enormously with age, fitness, medication, and chronic stress; applying a fixed cut-off would be misleading. Instead, the app establishes a personal resting baseline for each partner at the start of each session.

Baseline collection procedure

  1. Both partners sit quietly for 2–5 minutes while wearing the sensors.
  2. The therapist clicks "set baseline — A" and "set baseline — B" when ready.
  3. The app uses up to 5 minutes of buffered R–R data. It slides a 60-second sub-window in 30-second steps to generate multiple samples of each metric, giving a distribution rather than a single point estimate.
  4. For each metric (heart rate, RMSSD, HF power, coherence, respiration rate), the baseline stores the mean and standard deviation across sub-windows.
  5. Live values are then expressed as z-scores: (live − mean) ÷ SD.

The baseline indicator in the session header confirms when both baselines are set. Baselines can be cleared and reset mid-session (e.g., if a partner needed to move around before settling) using the "clear — A / B" buttons.

Timing recommendation Allow at least 2 minutes of quiet sitting before setting the baseline — sensors need time to settle and the nervous system needs time to shift toward resting tone. The state description and activation index are not available until the baseline is set.

§ 10Recovery Mode — Guided Breathing

The recovery screen is a standalone physiological break tool. It provides paced breathing with visual animation, ambient pink noise, and live coherence biofeedback. The therapist navigates there by clicking "recovery mode →" or pressing r; return to the session view with the "← back" link or Escape.

Breathing patterns

PatternPhases (in / hold / out / hold)RateRationale
Coherence 5.5 s in / 5.5 s out 5.5 breaths/min Near the cardiovascular resonance frequency — maximizes RSA and coherence [13].
Box 4 s / 4 s / 4 s / 4 s ~4 breaths/min Military-derived protocol; equal-phase structure supports focus and stabilization.
4-7-8 4 s / 7 s / 8 s ~3 breaths/min Extended exhalation activates the parasympathetic system; long hold builds CO₂ tolerance [14].

The breathing animation uses an SVG arc and an expanding circle. The arc fills clockwise over the inhale phase and resets at the start of each new cycle, giving an intuitive visual target. A countdown shows the remaining seconds in the current phase.

Ambient pink noise

The optional ambient sound is generated entirely in the browser using the Web Audio API. A 20-second stereo pink noise buffer is computed using the Voss-McCartney algorithm — a sequence of summed white noise generators at octave-spaced update rates that produces the characteristic 1/f power spectral density of natural sounds. The buffer is low-pass filtered at 700 Hz and looped continuously with a 100 ms crossfade to avoid audible clicks at the loop boundary.

Pink noise (rather than white noise or nature recordings) is used because its 1/f power distribution closely matches natural environmental soundscapes and has been shown to support relaxation and attentional defocusing [15].

Readiness indicator

When both partners' activation scores drop below 35 and remain there, a green readiness block appears showing the elapsed calm time. This is the signal that genuine physiological recovery has occurred and conversation can safely resume.

§ 11Interpretation Guide for Clinicians

At the start of a session

  • Give sensors 1–2 minutes to stabilize before setting the baseline.
  • Signal quality dots should be green (≥90%) before proceeding. If yellow or red, check sensor placement and contact.
  • The state description should read "low activation, stable" if the person is genuinely at rest. If it reads "moderate" at rest, the baseline may have been captured during mild anxiety — consider resetting.

During a session

  • Watch the direction indicator first — "rising" on both partners during a difficult topic is an early warning, even if the scores are still moderate.
  • The coherence tile often drops before heart rate rises significantly — loss of organized heart rhythm can precede conscious awareness of stress.
  • A flooded badge means heart rate is more than 10% above the personal baseline. At this level, Gottman's research suggests the person cannot reliably engage empathically. Call a break before the conversation continues to escalate.
  • The dyadic panel is most useful when both partners are still regulated — it can reveal who is physiologically leading the escalation before either individual reaches flooding threshold.

During a recovery break

  • Both partners should follow the same breathing pattern together.
  • Coherence typically takes 2–3 minutes to rise noticeably. Expect the first cycle or two to show little change.
  • The calm-zone timer is a better guide than wall clock time — wait for it to appear and accumulate before resuming conversation.
  • If a partner's coherence is not rising after 5+ minutes of paced breathing, they may be too cognitively engaged with the unresolved conflict. A brief structured distraction (a simple task, a change of room) may be needed.

§ 12Limitations and Important Caveats

This is a research prototype, not a validated clinical instrument. The thresholds, weights, and algorithms described here represent reasonable implementations of published methods. They have not been validated in a clinical trial with couples therapy populations. Do not use scores as diagnostic criteria or to make clinical decisions without therapist judgment.

PPG vs. ECG

Consumer heart rate monitors use photoplethysmography (PPG) — an optical sensor — rather than electrocardiography (ECG). PPG-derived R–R intervals have higher noise and are more susceptible to movement artifact. The ectopic filter helps, but during vigorous movement or if the sensor is loose, signal quality will degrade.

No respiration sensor

Respiration rate is derived from the heart rate signal, not measured directly. RSA-based respiration rate estimation is unreliable during continuous speech, irregular breathing, or rates below 9 breaths/min. The HF power metric similarly loses its "vagal activity" interpretation during slow breathing.

Individual differences

HRV declines with age, is suppressed by beta-blockers and other cardiac medications, varies with fitness level, time of day, and caffeine intake. The personalized baseline adjusts for the individual's state at the start of that session, but does not account for day-to-day or medication-driven variation in the absolute values.

Dyadic synchrony interpretation

The dyadic coupling signal should be treated as exploratory, not diagnostic. Cross-correlation measures statistical association, not causal influence. Two people in the same room will show some synchrony simply from shared environmental events. Without a surrogate comparison baseline, the clinical meaning of a specific r value cannot be established rigorously.

Emotional interpretation

The app describes body state, not emotion. "High activation, rising" means the body is in a mobilized state — it does not distinguish excitement from anger, or effort from anxiety. The therapist's relational attunement remains the primary interpretive instrument.
